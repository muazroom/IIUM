Component 1 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Learner
Age, grade, language, prior Islamic knowledge, learning difficulties and strengths
HHow to Analyse
  • Identify learner characteristics: age, grade, linguistic background, and prior Islamic knowledge.
  • Identify learning difficulties, misconceptions, and academic strengths.
  • Analyse current achievement through exam results, class tests, and in-class performance records.
  • Explore motivation, interest, and attitudes towards Islamic Education and the language of instruction.
  • Assess learners' proficiency in English or the instructional medium used in class.
MMethods
  • Review exam results, school records, and previous assessments for patterns of achievement.
  • Administer student questionnaires on interest, perceived difficulty, relevance, and preferred activities.
  • Conduct short diagnostic tests on key topics such as Seerah, Fiqh basics, and Quranic understanding.
  • Carry out classroom observations focused on engagement, participation, and language use.
  • Hold informal conversations or short focus groups with students about their learning experience.
SSignificance
  • Places the learner at the centre of all curriculum and module design decisions.
  • Ensures learning outcomes, content difficulty, language level, and strategies match real learners.
  • Prevents designing for an imagined "average" student rather than the actual cohort.
  • Aligns language level and teaching strategies with the students' true proficiency.
  • If neglected, the curriculum may be theoretically sound but practically unsuitable in the classroom.

Component 2 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Teacher
Qualifications, content knowledge, pedagogy, language, assessment literacy, ICT, and development needs
HHow to Analyse
  • Review professional qualifications, teaching experience, and training history in Islamic Education.
  • Assess content knowledge in Qur'an, Hadith, Fiqh, Sirah, and Akhlaq relevant to the curriculum level.
  • Evaluate use of student-centred pedagogy, questioning, group work, and formative assessment.
  • Assess language proficiency and ability to scaffold content for multilingual learners.
  • Identify beliefs about curriculum aims, openness to change, and perceived barriers in teaching.
  • Determine professional development needs and preferred formats for capacity building.
MMethods
  • Teacher needs-analysis questionnaire covering content confidence, strategies, ICT use, and support needs.
  • Semi-structured interviews with Islamic Education teachers and heads of department.
  • Classroom observations using a structured checklist on activities, questioning, and engagement.
  • Document review of lesson plans, teaching slides, worksheets, and assessments prepared by teachers.
  • Short reflective prompts or informal conversations about daily teaching challenges and successes.
SSignificance
  • Teachers are the primary implementers; their capacity shapes what students actually experience.
  • Prevents designing strategies or materials that teachers cannot realistically deliver.
  • Identifies training, mentoring, and resources that must accompany the new module.
  • Reveals the gap between intended curriculum design and actual classroom practice.
  • Ensures consistent and faithful implementation across different classes and teachers.

Component 3 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Stakeholders' Needs and Expectations
Teachers, parents, school leaders, community figures, and alumni
HHow to Analyse
  • Clarify what each stakeholder group expects from Islamic Education: knowledge, character, exam results, identity, and life skills.
  • Identify perceived strengths and weaknesses of the existing curriculum from their perspectives.
  • Explore hopes, worries, and suggestions for improvement from different groups.
  • Compare expectations across groups to identify consensus, tensions, or competing priorities.
  • Assess how much support or resistance a new curriculum approach might receive from each group.
MMethods
  • Semi-structured interviews with school leaders and heads of Islamic Education on strategic aims.
  • Focus group discussions or individual interviews with Islamic Education teachers.
  • Parent questionnaires or small focus groups conducted during school events or online.
  • Consultation with community or religious leaders such as mosque committees and local scholars.
  • Conversations with alumni about how useful their school Islamic Education was after graduation.
SSignificance
  • Ensures the curriculum is socially legitimate and supported by the school and wider community.
  • Captures strong parental and community expectations about values, identity, and character formation.
  • Prevents resistance or low uptake of a well-designed module that stakeholders do not trust or want.
  • Strengthens the credibility and ummah-responsiveness of the curriculum research and design.
  • Builds ownership and cooperation before implementation begins.

Component 4 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Facilities
Classrooms, library, school compound, internet access, and technology
HHow to Analyse
  • Map all available physical spaces: classrooms, prayer rooms, library, outdoor areas, and multi-purpose halls.
  • Evaluate digital resources: projectors, computers, tablets, internet stability, and learning platforms.
  • Assess the condition, accessibility, and reliability of each facility for regular teaching use.
  • Relate facilities to potential teaching methods such as ICT-based tasks, outdoor projects, or group work.
  • Identify equity issues: whether all students have equal access to devices or internet outside school.
MMethods
  • Conduct a facilities audit or walkthrough of the school using a structured checklist.
  • Discuss access rules, maintenance schedules, and timetabling with administration and ICT staff.
  • Review library catalogues, digital subscriptions, and existing online learning platforms.
  • Observe how current facilities are used during Islamic Education lessons in real time.
  • Survey teachers on which facilities they actually use, which are broken, and which they need.
SSignificance
  • Defines the realistic boundaries of what learning activities and methods can be planned.
  • Prevents designing modules that depend on unavailable or unreliable technology or spaces.
  • Supports the selection of ambitious yet feasible instructional strategies in the Design phase.
  • Helps maximise learning impact within the infrastructure that actually exists.
  • Guides budget and resource requests where gaps are identified.

Component 5 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Curriculum
Existing materials, textbooks, lesson plans, schemes of work, and assessments
HHow to Analyse
  • Examine current aims, learning outcomes, and topic coverage in the Islamic Education syllabus.
  • Analyse textbooks and teacher-made materials for content balance, language level, and relevance to students' lives.
  • Review lesson plans and schemes of work to see how content is sequenced and taught week by week.
  • Evaluate assessment tools for alignment with outcomes, weighting, and cognitive demand.
  • Benchmark the current curriculum against selected comparator curricula from other countries or schools.
MMethods
  • Document analysis of national curriculum standards, school syllabi, and schemes of work.
  • Content analysis of textbooks and student materials using a structured coding framework.
  • Review of lesson plans, teaching slides, worksheets, and in-class activities.
  • Analysis of exam papers and other assessments: weightage, question types, and Bloom's levels.
  • Curriculum mapping grid across selected comparator curricula to identify similarities and gaps.
SSignificance
  • Identifies what is already strong and should be retained in the redesigned module.
  • Pinpoints real gaps such as weak application, outdated examples, or low higher-order thinking.
  • Prevents unnecessary replacement of effective elements and wasteful duplication.
  • Links directly to comparative analysis to show where improvement is most needed.
  • Provides concrete evidence for why the new module must take a different direction.

Component 6 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Constraints
Financial, time, policy, and human resource limitations
HHow to Analyse
  • Determine the actual teaching time available: periods per week, number of weeks, and exam interruptions.
  • Identify budget limitations for books, printing, digital tools, excursions, and guest speakers.
  • Map policy constraints including national exam requirements, compulsory topics, and school rules.
  • Note human resource constraints: number of qualified Islamic Education teachers and support staff.
  • Assess whether the school calendar, co-curricular demands, and external exams reduce effective time.
MMethods
  • Review school timetables, academic calendars, and term schedules in detail.
  • Analyse budget documents and administrative reports on resource allocation.
  • Interview principals or heads of department about financial and staffing realities.
  • Review official policy documents, examination regulations, and Ministry directives.
  • Map the teaching load of each Islamic Education teacher across classes and levels.
SSignificance
  • Sets the realistic scope and depth for every decision in the Design phase.
  • Prevents the creation of excellent but unimplementable curriculum designs.
  • Demonstrates to examiners and reviewers that the design is grounded and sustainable.
  • Ensures learning outcomes and activities are calibrated to real teaching time.
  • Highlights where advocacy or resource requests may be needed before implementation.

Component 7 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Global and Societal Changes
Digitalisation, globalisation, pluralism, secular influences, and moral challenges
HHow to Analyse
  • Identify major global trends affecting Muslim youth: digital media, globalisation, pluralism, and secularism.
  • Review how these trends are discussed in current Islamic education literature and policy documents.
  • Consider new learning needs created by these changes: digital ethics, media literacy, and interfaith skills.
  • Examine whether the current curriculum content reflects, ignores, or contradicts these realities.
  • Assess the gap between what students face in society and what the curriculum prepares them for.
MMethods
  • Systematic literature review on contemporary challenges facing Islamic Education globally.
  • Review of international reports and scholarly work on youth, religion, and social change.
  • Thematic analysis of how global issues appear, or are absent, in existing curriculum materials.
  • Analysis of news, social media trends, and youth behaviour studies in the national context.
  • Consultation with counsellors or pastoral staff on student issues linked to global trends.
SSignificance
  • Keeps the curriculum responsive to the lived realities of students rather than frozen in the past.
  • Justifies the inclusion of themes such as social media ethics and Islamic digital citizenship.
  • Positions Islamic Education as a living, adaptive subject relevant to contemporary life.
  • Strengthens the academic argument for reform by connecting it to documented global pressures.
  • Ensures graduates are prepared for moral and intellectual challenges beyond the classroom.

Component 8 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
National Philosophy and Educational Policy
Education philosophy, curriculum frameworks, and Islamic Education standards
HHow to Analyse
  • Study the national education philosophy and specific policy documents related to Islamic Education.
  • Identify key concepts and phrases such as holistic, balanced, integrated, and 21st-century skills.
  • Map how far the current curriculum and classroom practice embody these stated ideals.
  • Identify misalignments between policy ambitions and what is actually taught and assessed.
  • Examine recent curriculum reform documents and competency frameworks for Islamic Education.
MMethods
  • Document analysis of national philosophy statements and curriculum frameworks.
  • Mapping of policy aims against current curriculum content, outcomes, and assessments.
  • Interviews with school leaders about how they interpret and operationalise national policy.
  • Review of Ministry circulars, examination specifications, and competency standards.
  • Comparison of stated national graduate profiles with current module learning outcomes.
SSignificance
  • Aligns the redesigned module with officially stated national aims and directives.
  • Strengthens the policy legitimacy and institutional acceptability of the curriculum design.
  • Demonstrates that the design responds to national goals, not only local preferences.
  • Provides a framework for justifying competency-based and values-integrated content.
  • Reduces the risk of non-approval or marginalisation within the school system.

Component 9 of 9 ADDIE — Analysis Phase
Industry, Societal & Ummah Needs · Community & Institutional Context
Graduate profiles, societal contribution, school ethos, and community realities
HHow to Analyse
  • Clarify knowledge, skills, and dispositions needed for meaningful contribution to society and the ummah.
  • Consider expectations from higher education and the job market regarding Islamic values and generic skills.
  • Examine community and ummah-level discourses on what is lacking in current Muslim youth.
  • Understand the school community: socio-economic background, religious practices, diversity, and local issues.
  • Analyse school mission, ethos, discipline culture, and how Islamic Education is positioned institutionally.
MMethods
  • Literature review on Islamic Education aims, desired graduate profiles, and youth development.
  • Consultation with community leaders, employers, or higher-education representatives on graduate attributes.
  • Analysis of national development plans and Islamic organisations' mission statements.
  • Review of school vision, mission, strategic plans, and promotional materials.
  • Interviews with school leaders and observation of school culture including assemblies and events.
  • Informal conversations with teachers and parents about community character and expectations.
SSignificance
  • Connects school Islamic Education to life beyond school and to the needs of the wider ummah.
  • Supports the inclusion of critical thinking, communication, and ethical decision-making.
  • Positions the module strategically within the school mission and community ecosystem.
  • Shows how the curriculum can reinforce strong community support or compensate where it is weak.
  • Strengthens the case for a holistic, future-oriented Islamic Education curriculum.
