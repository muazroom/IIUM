Design phase notes

From analysis into a teaching blueprint.

The Design phase in ADDIE translates the findings from analysis into a structured plan for teaching and learning. It clarifies what will be taught, how it will be taught, how learning will be assessed, and what conditions must be considered before development begins.

What to plan

Outcomes, content structure, teaching strategies, assessments, delivery mode, resources, and technology.

How to design

Move step by step from outcomes to learning journey, methods, media, assessment, and feedback.

What to consider

Learner needs, context, constraints, alignment, differentiation, accessibility, and evaluation from the start.

Section one

What to plan and design

Focus on building the blueprint for teaching and learning before any materials are produced.

01
P

Plan

  • Learning outcomes/objectives: Write clear outcomes that are SMART and aligned with identified needs, philosophy, and curriculum aims.
  • Content structure: Decide units, topics, sequence, and estimated time for each part of the module.
  • Teaching–learning strategies: Choose suitable approaches such as lectures, discussions, cooperative learning, projects, and digital tools.
  • Assessment plan: Plan formative and summative tasks, rubrics, and the link between each task and each objective.
  • Delivery mode and resources: Decide whether delivery will be face-to-face, online, or blended, and identify required materials, media, and technology.
M

Meaning

  • This stage does not yet produce the actual worksheets, slides, or videos.
  • Instead, it creates the blueprint that guides later development.
  • A strong design phase prevents confusion, duplication, and weak alignment in later stages.
  • It also ensures that teaching decisions are intentional rather than improvised.
A

Advice

  • Keep each planned item directly linked to the analysis findings.
  • Avoid overloading the blueprint with too many objectives or activities.
  • Think of the design phase as architectural planning: clear enough for others to build from it later.
Section two

How to design

Turn analysis results into a structured plan through a logical step-by-step design process.

02
1

Start from outcomes

  • Translate the needs identified in the analysis phase into clear learning outcomes for each unit and lesson.
  • Make sure each outcome states what learners should know, do, or demonstrate by the end of instruction.
  • Check that the outcomes reflect both curriculum requirements and broader educational philosophy.
2

Map learning journey

  • Design a sequence of lessons and activities that move from simple to complex and from guided to more independent learning.
  • Outline or storyboard the learning flow so each lesson builds logically on the previous one.
  • Plan where prior knowledge will be activated, where new ideas will be introduced, and where application will happen.
3

Choose methods, media, and assessment

  • Match teaching strategies and media to each outcome and learner profile.
  • Plan how learners will demonstrate learning through tasks, performances, reflections, or tests.
  • Decide how and when feedback will be given at each stage of the learning process.
Section three

What to take into consideration

Good design is not only about ideas; it must also respond to real learners, context, and future evaluation.

03
L

Learners and context

  • Learner characteristics: language level, prior knowledge, motivation, learning preferences, and special educational needs.
  • Context and constraints: timetable, class size, facilities, technology access, and teacher workload.
  • The design must remain realistic within the actual school context.
A

Alignment and accessibility

  • Alignment: Ensure coherence with national curriculum, school vision, Islamic values, and the needs statement.
  • Accessibility and differentiation: Plan support for weaker learners and extension for stronger learners.
  • Use varied activities, clear instructions, and multiple ways for learners to participate and demonstrate understanding.
E

Evaluation from the start

  • Think early about how the design will be checked during and after implementation.
  • Plan data sources such as student work, assessment results, teacher reflection, and student voice.
  • This makes the design phase more iterative and prepares the way for evaluation and revision.
